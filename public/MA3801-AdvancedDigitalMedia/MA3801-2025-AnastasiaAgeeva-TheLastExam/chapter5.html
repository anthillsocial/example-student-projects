Chapter 3: Expanding the beast's cultural knowledge

One type of poetry I found particularly fun to play around with was the Japanese Haiku. As the structure of this art form is very specific and constrained, it became an interesting challenge to see how much the AI could do with the limited word count.

At first I gave the bot freedom to improvise the content and you are not going to believe the theme it chose…

Once again, the output was quite generic but fit the brief otherwise, using the correct syllable count. The only problem, aside from the theme, seems to be that the AI struggles to connect the lines into a coherent story, which is something evident throughout all of the poetry styles, especially when minimal instructions are given. What often ends up happening is that the machine produces a piece of text where each stanza, pair, or even a single line, can be shuffled around in whatever order and, ignoring the rhyme, would make equally as much, or evidently little, sense.

After the first try, I decided to narrow down the content, basing it off of a poem I wrote:
“Truth sits in the past,
the present holds the questions,
future is a lie.”


Both results were decent but were quite meaningless compared to my piece, again struggling to create a united message. I then moved over to the pro version and asked it to specifically include all of the necessary keywords that feature in my poem, however the results were even more nonsensical.

Even though I was convinced my Haiku was quite simple and intuitive, no matter how much I tried to push the chatbot into the right direction, it always missed, even when given all of the necessary ingredients. It was only when I essentially explained the entire piece to it that it finally managed to make something close to my idea, but even then, it couldn’t help but mention the silence (???).
