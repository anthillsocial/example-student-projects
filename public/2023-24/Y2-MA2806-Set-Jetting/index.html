What is the purpose of this site?

With the UK box office alone generating a revenue of £986 million a year, the scale of the global film industry is huge. The Film industry has a massive impact on local economies, the environment and tourism across the globe. This website highlights its global impact through the popular trend of Set Jetting - the act of traveling to destinations that are featured on High End TV or feature films. This trend has boosted tourism in many places, which has both positive and negative effects to an area. The map displays these iconic locations and the films that bring them to life, for example the cult franchise Lord of the rings and rising TV shows such as the White Lotus. As well as the map look though the case studies highlighting the positives and negatives of this trend which considered who it targets and the wider impact it can have on the environment and culture.

The Beach: 20th Century Fox 2000, Thailand.
Although there are many positive economic impacts to set jetting more likely than not there is a negative environmental impact to counter act it. The Beach directed by Danny Boyle starting Leonardo DiCaprio filmed in 2000 is an example of set getting gone wrong. The film centres around a young traveller who wants to get off the beaten track and finds himself living on a secrete Island with a small community. This island is referred to as “the Beach” but its Maya Bay, on Ko Phi Phi Lee a national Park in Thailand. The beach was always a popular tourist spot but mostly for Thai holiday makers, however, the Beach put it on the international Map. Only accessible by boat visitors went from 1000 a day to an average of 5000 a day. The boats with their propellers and ankers would come to shore destroying the corral and marina life below. This Over population of the island led to the Thai governments closing it in October 2018 to give the island time to recover from the excessive tourism. Local authorities filed a civil lawsuit in late 1999 against Thai government agencies, 20th Century Fox and a Thai film coordinator, seeking 100m baht in compensation for environmental damage. The island reopens after the pandemic in 2022 and since then has put restriction on tourists and boats visiting the island and now close it for a period of the year to allow the Maya Bay to try and recover.

White Lotus: HBO, TV Series 2021, Hawaii.
The White Lotus became very popular when it was released post COVID as it provided the idyllic dream holiday and escape everyone had been craving after the global lockdown. The film was based on and shot in real Four Seasons Resort Maui at Wailea. Made under unique circumstance as the cast and crew lived and worked at the resort as it was closed during covid so had full range of the location to shoot the film and worked in their own bubble. After the HBO series was released this boosted tourism in the area which led to a 386 percent increase in availability checks at the property. As the resort is a Five star, very expenses, exclusive destination with rooms fetching up to $26,000 a night this location isn’t affordable to all and shows how set jetting has hobby or experience isn’t inclusive, thus segregated people by economic status and class.

The Hobbit & Lord of the Rings: Hobbiton Film Setm Matamata, New Zealand.
The Hobbit and Lord of the Rings franchise features a high production value with it being a billion-dollar franchise and still counting, and therefore this is shown in the choice of filming location. As a result, it has benefitted New Zealand, with them receiving $1.5 billion in economic benefits from the franchise (Clark, 2010). However, there is also concerns from those living in New Zealand, with worries that the country attracts too many tourists. According to a report in 2019 by Tourism New Zealand, issues such as traffic congestion, overcrowding at tourist destinations, lack of infrastructure, and environmental degradation are making tensions between residents and tourists worse. (The Tourism New Zealand Report, 2019).

Friends: Warner Brothers, Hollywood/New York City, 1924 – 2004.
Traditionally a lot of film and TV shows are predominantly shot in studios. They are purpose-built sets that are created for the show and then destroyed after production. However, when films and TV generate a cult following, the idea of the show can live on and generate a lot of revenue for the Production company and tourism in the county, even after the show ends. This is the case for the hit tv series Friends, an iconic 90’s shows running for 10 years it build up a huge fan base and continues to do so. Although the show ended 20 years ago peoples still make it apart of their experience when they visit New York, where the film was set. “The friends Experience” in Manhattan New York allows visitors to see the set, walk though character Monica’s apartment and get a coffee at the iconic Central Perks coffee shop featured in the show which in the real world is called “The Little Owl”. The experience is created by Warner Brothers who have a lot of knowledge in converting their programmes and shows into experiences as they have done the same with film franchise Harry Potter. Although this would be classed as “Set Jetting” it begs the question, is the real purpose of tourism being lost? People now flock to countries to see sets of their favourite shows which, often are fabricated and fake doubles based somewhere else in the world. When people base their idea of a destination on a show this can often depict, and idealised staged destination and tourists lack the experience of a cultural and authentic experience when visiting that country/destination.

Harry Potter: UK 2001.
Harry Potter has been an evident source of economic growth for the UK’s tourism, it has been assumed by a professor at the London School of Economics said that Harry Potter was ‘worth £4billion to the UK economy’ (Cox, 2017). Harry Potter has been filmed over various sites within the United Kingdom, such as the Bodleian Library in Oxford, Durham Cathedral, King’s Cross and Goathland station in North Yorkshire. Therefore, it is hard to estimate the data increase of tourism with before and after statistics. However, through looking at the number of visitors attending Harry Potter Studios in Watford before and post Covid-19 pandemic, it was evident they saw a huge increase due to people being tied to their homes watching the franchise through their streaming subscriptions.
